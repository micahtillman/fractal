About this app

The Fractalline app is a tool for exploring mathematical and algorithmic line art.

It has two sides (or modes), one devoted to Lindenmayer systems, and the other devoted to prime factors and the number line.

You can use the app in both modes to discover and/or create fractals.

Lindenmayer Systems

A Lindenmayer system needs two things: symbols and rules. [For more info, see Wikipedia and a book co-written by Lindenmayer]

Symbols

On the symbols side, there is:

  1. a set of symbols you are allowed to use
  2. a seed (a particular symbol [or sequence of symbols] that you want to start with).

For example, if I wanted to make a Lindenmayer system, I might choose:

  1. "f," "g," "l," and "r" as the set of symbols that I'm allowed to use, and
  2. "f" as the seed I want to start with.
Rules

On the rules side, there is:

  1. a set of replacement rules (one for each symbol), and
  2. a set of interpretation rules (one for each symbol).

For example, I might use the following replacement rules for my Lindenmayer system:

  • "f" is replaced by "flg" (if you read an "f," erase it and write an "f," "l," and "g" in its place)
  • "g" is replaced by "frg" (if you read a "g," erase it and write an "f," "r," and "g" in its place)
  • "r" is replaced by "r" (if you read an "r", erase it and write another "r" in its place)
  • "l" is replaced by "l" (if you read an "l", erase it and write another "l" in its place)

Notice that there has to be one -- but only one -- replacement rule for every symbol you're allowed to use in the system.

In addition to having a replacement rule for each symbol, a Lindenmayer system needs an interpretation rule for each symbol.

So, imagine that we use use the following interpretation rules:

  • "f" means "move forward one step while drawing a line"
  • "g" means "move forward one step while drawing a line"
  • "r" means "turn right 90 degrees"
  • "l" means "turn right 90 degrees"

Just like with replacement rules, there has to be one -- but only one -- interpretation rule for every symbol you're allowed to use in the system.

Example: Applying Replacement Rules

So, imagine I start with the seed, "f." I would read that "f" and check the replacement rule for "f" in my list of replacement rules.

The replacement rule for "f" says to erase the "f" and write "flg" instead. So, I would do just that:

  •"f" becomes "flg"

Next, imagine that I re-read the seed. Where it used to be one letter ("f"), it is now three: "flg." I start by reading the "f" in "flg." I look up the replacement rule for "f" and see that "f" is replaced by "flg."

  •"flg" becomes "flglg"

The next letter after the "f" (which we just replaced) was an "l," so I check the replacement rule for "l." Turns out, "l" gets replaced by itself. So:

  •"flglg" becomes "flglg"

Finally, I read the "g". The replacement rule for "g" says to replace it with "frg." So I do that.

  •"flglg" becomes "flglfrg"

After two "readings," the seed for my Lindenmayer system has grown from a single "f" into the string of symbols: "frgrflg."
  1. Seed starts as: "f"
  2. After reading 1, seed is: "flg" (because "f" turns into "flg")
  3. After reading 2, seed is: "flglfrg" (because "f" turns into "flg," "l" remains itself, and "g" turns into "frg")
And we could keep going. If we read the seed a third time:
  • "flglfrg" becomes "flglglfrg"
  • "flglglfrg" becomes "flglglfrg"
  • "flglglfrg" becomes "flglfrglfrg"
  • "flglfrglflg" becomes "flglfrglfrg"
  • "flglfrglfrg" becomes "flglfrglflgrg"
  • "flglfrglflgrg" becomes "flglfrglflgrg"
  • "flglfrglflgrg" becomes "flglfrglflgrfrg"
So, after a third reading, our seed would be: "flglfrglflgrfrg"
  1. Seed starts as: "f"
  2. After reading 1, seed is: "flg"
  3. After reading 2, seed is: "flglfrg"
  4. After reading 3, seed is: "flglfrglflgrfrg"

And we could keep re-reading our string of symbols over and over, as many times as we want. Each time we read through the seed and apply the replacement rules is called an "iteration."

We can put the seed through as many readings ("iterations") as we want, making it as long as we want.

Example: Applying Interpretation Rules

Then we would use the interpretation rules as follows:

  1. "Grow" (iterate) the seed as many times as we wish, using the replacement rules.
  2. Read the "all grown up" version of the seed, one letter at a time (but this time, don't do any replacing).
  3. As you read each letter, do what the interpretation rule for each letter tells you to do.

So, let's try it out on the seed we grew above:

  1. flglfrglflgrfrg: "f" means "move forward one step while drawing a line." So, we draw a short line.
  2. flglfrglflgrfrg: "l" means "turn left 90 degrees." So, we do.
  3. flglfrglflgrfrg: "g" means the same as "f." We take one step forward, in the new direction we are now facing, drawing a line.
  4. flglfrglflgrfrg: We turn left 90 degrees again.
  5. flglfrglflgrfrg: We take one step forward, drawing a line.
  6. flglfrglflgrfrg: "r" means "turn right 90 degrees." So, we do so.
  7. flglfrglflgrfrg: We take one step forward, drawing a line.
  8. flglfrglflgrfrg: We turn left 90 degrees again.
  9. flglfrglflgrfrg: We take one step forward, drawing a line.
  10. flglfrglflgrfrg: We turn left 90 degrees again.
  11. flglfrglflgrfrg: We take one step forward, drawing a line.
  12. flglfrglflgrfrg: We turn right 90 degrees again.
  13. flglfrglflgrfrg: We take one step forward, drawing a line.
  14. flglfrglflgrfrg: We turn right 90 degrees again.
  15. flglfrglflgrfrg: We take one step forward, drawing a line.

Prime Factors and the Number Line

The "natural numbers" (or "counting numbers," or "positive integers") are the numbers 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, and so on. We usually think of them as being evenly spaced along "the number line":

Composites and Primes

We like to think of these numbers as divided into different types. For example, every natural number is either even or odd. However, the division we'll be interested in here is that between composites and primes.

A composite number is a natural number larger than 1 that you can divide into equal chunks that are also natural numbers larger than 1. For example, 4 is a natural number larger than 1, and can be divided into two equal chunks -- 2 and 2 -- that are also natural numbers larger than 1. Similarly, 12 is a natural number larger than 1 that can be divided into equal chunks -- 4, 4, and 4 -- that are also natural numbers larger than 1.

In contrast, a prime number is a natural number larger than 1 that you can't divide into equal chunks that are also natural numbers larger than 1. For example, 5 is a natural number larger than 1, but there's no way of splitting it up into equal chunks that are also natural numbers larger than 1. If you split it into two equal chunks, you'd get 2.5 and 2.5, neither of which are natural numbers. If you split it into 3 equal chunks, you'd get 1.666..., 1.666..., and 1.666..., which aren't natural numbers. And if you split it into 5 equal chunks, you'd get 1, 1, 1, 1, and 1, which are all natural numbers, but aren't larger than 1.

Prime Factorizations

One thing mathematicians figured out long ago is that every composite number can be created by multiplying together a unique set of prime numbers. For example, 14 is composite, but can be produced by multiplying one 2 times one 7 (i.e., 21 x 71 = 14). However, if you change that set by including other prime numbers (e.g., 21 x 71 x 111), or changing the number of prime numbers that are already present (e.g., 25 x 74), you'll get different composite numbers.

The unique set of prime numbers that, produce a particular composite number when multiplied together, is called the "prime factorization" of that composite number. What distinguishes one prime factorization from another is (1) which prime numbers it includes, and (2) how many of each of those prime numbers it includes. (The prime factorization of 2,250, for example, is 21 x 32 x 53. It contains one 2, two 3s, and three 5s.)

Once you have realized that every natural number will either be composite or prime, you might start to ask questions like:

  • Is there an easy way to tell whether a given natural number is prime?
  • If a number is composite, is there an easy way of figuring out what its prime factorization is?
  • If I know the prime factorization of one number (call it "n"), is there an easy way to figure out the prime factorization of the next number (n+1)?
  • If I know which numbers were prime, up to a particular point on the number line, is there a way to predict what the next prime number will be?
  • Or, to put the previous question in another way, do the prime numbers follow a pattern? Are they distributed along the number line in a predictable way? Or is their placement entirely random?
  • Etc.
Sieves

Perhaps the simplest way to find out which numbers are prime, and which aren't, is to use what is called a "sieve." A sieve is just an algorithm (a series of instructions to follow) that crosses out all the composite numbers along a particular part of the number line. Since all the composites will get crossed out, only the primes will be left.

The Sieve of Eratosthenes, for example, says to create a list of numbers. Then, start going through the list. Whenever you find a prime number, cross out all its multiples in the list. Whenever you get to a number you haven't crossed out, you know that number must be the next prime.

For example, imagine we write down the natural numbers from 1 through 100. We skip 1, because it is neither prime nor composite. The first number we encounter is 2, and it hasn't been crossed out. So, it must be prime. We then cross out all the multiples of 2 (e.g., we cross out 4, 6, 8, 10, 12, . . .).

After crossing out all the multiples of 2, we move on to the next number in the list: 3. We haven't crossed out 3 yet, so it must be prime. And that means we now need to cross out all the multiples of 3 (e.g., 6, 9, 12, 15, etc.)

After crossing out all the multiples of 3, the next number we come to is 4. But 4 has already been crossed out, since it's a multiple of 2. So, we go on to 5. As it turns out, 5 hasn't been crossed out yet, so it i must be prime. We, therefore, cross out all the multiples of 5 (10, 15, 20, 25, etc.).

Once we've crossed out all the multiples of 5, we move on to 6. Well, what we actually do is notice that 6 has already been crossed out (as a multiple of both 2 and 3), and thus we skip it and go to the next number that hasn't been crossed out: 7. Since it hasn't been crossed out yet, it must be prime.

Filling in the table is relatively simple. First, list all the natural numbers from 1 up to m along the top (where m is whatever maximum number you want to deal with; in the table above, m is 32).

Second, list all the natural numbers from 2 up to m/2 along the side of the table (in the table above, m/2 is 16).

Third, work your way down the table, one row at a time. To fill in each row, you will need to create an ordered sequence ("array") of numbers, using the following series of steps:

For any natural number n:
  1. Create an array containing nothing but a single 0 (This is "the current array.")
  2. Create duplicates of the current array until you have n copies thereof (including the original).
  3. Append these n arrays to each other (linking them together, end-to-end) to form a single, larger array.
  4. Increase the final element in the new, larger array, by 1. The array in this state is now "the current array."
  5. If the current array is long enough for your purposes, stop. Otherwise, go to step 2.

Filtering Out the Composite Numbers

Once you have the table filled in, you might notice some patterns.

  • Every row starts with a string of one or more 0s, followed by a 1.
  • The first 1 in the row for n is always in the nth column. (The first 1 in the "3" row is in the "3" column. The first 1 in the "11" row is in the "11" column. Etc.)
  • If the column of numbers above the first 1 in row n contains only zeros, then n is a prime number.
  • If the column of numbers above the first 1 in row n contains any non-zero numbers, then n is composite.

And now we can read off the prime factorization of each number by reading down the numbers in the column below it. Below "18," for example, there is a 1 in the "2" row, and a 2 in the "3" row. The eagle-eyed among you might have noticed that some numbers do not have the correct prime factorization in the table above. Specifically, the prime numbers larger than 13 do not have a "1" below them in the table below, as they ought to. For example, the prime factorization of 17 is 171, so "17" ought to have a 1 below it in the row for "17."

The problem is that we cut the table off at row 16, and then even got rid of the rows for 16, 15, and 14. Fortunately, however, this isn't actually a problem. We don't need all those rows to know that a given number is prime.

As it turns out, we can read off the prime factorizations for any composite number c if we have included all the rows from 2 up to c/2. Furthermore, we can know that some natural number n is prime after only filling in the rows from 2 up to the square root of n (this is a fact common to all sieves, I believe). If all the rows in the column below n contain 0s, from the row for "2" up to the row for square root of n, then n is prime. And from knowing that a given n is prime, we automatically know that its prime factorization is n1.

How do I get rid of all the letters and boxes and whatnot that I don't want?

The fastest way is to click the image (with "Fractalline" and other "-line" words in it). A slower way is to empty all the textboxes. Whenever a letter is no longer being used in a textbox, the app will remove all references to it from the page.

How do I make a fractal?

You can either choose one from the menu that says, "Choose a fractal type" (in the Lindenmayer Mode of the app), or you can create your own. Before you try making your own, I would recommend picking a few from the menu, just to see how they are set up.

When you're ready to make your own fractal, you need to do six things:

  1. Make sure you're in the "Lindenmayer" mode, and have a blank slate to work with. (You can accomplish both by clicking the image at the top left of the page).
  2. Choose a letter to start with, and type it into the ". . . or start a string" textbox.
  3. A new line will appear with the letter you just typed, followed by "becomes" and a second textbox. Enter one or more letters into this new textbox.
  4. If you enter any letters other than the original one you picked, lines for those letters, with their own textboxes will pop up. Decide what letters to enter into those new boxes.
  5. Below the lines with textboxes, lines with dropdown menus for each letter will also appear. For each letter, use its menu to select the instruction/command/action it will represent.
  6. Click the "GO" button.

Alternatively, you can:

  1. Make sure you're in the "Lindenmayer" mode, and have a blank slate to work with. (You can accomplish both by clicking the image at the top left of the page).
  2. Click on the ". . . or start a string" textbox, but don't type anything.
  3. Click on the canvas and drag the mouse to draw a line.
  4. Click on one of the other textboxes that just appeared and draw one or more lines on the canvas.
  5. Repeat the previous step as many times as you like.
  6. Click the "GO" button.
I followed your instructions, and it didn't make a fractal. It made a mess, or just a straight line, or just a box, or something else that was boring.

If the app didn't produce a fractal, I'd recommend checking a few things:

  • Problem: It didn't draw anything.
    Potential Solution: Make sure to set at least one of the letters to mean "Step Forward" or "Step Back." If they are all set to mean "Do Nothing" or "Turn," the app won't have anything to draw.
  • Problem: It just drew a straight line.
    Potential Solution: Set at least one of the letters to mean "Turn Left" or "Turn Right," then set the degrees to turn to be something other than 0. (If the app doesn't interpret any of the letters as a turn, then it will just draw a straight line.)
    Another Potential Solution: Make sure the the "Iterations" textbox has a large enough number in it. In some Lindenmayer systems, the letters that mean "turn" don't show up in the instruction string until the system has gone through several iterations.
    Another Potential Solution: Make sure at least one of the "becomes" textboxes contains multiple letters. To get an interesting image, the string of instructions needs to grow, and it won't do that if every letter is replaced by a single other letter. To check whether the string of instructions is growing, click the "View Instruction String" button (which appears after you click the "GO" button).
  • Problem: It drew a big tangled mess.
    Potential Solution: Try different angles. I find that setting the turns to use 30, 60, and/or 120, or 45, 95, and/or 135, often works well.
  • Problem: It drew a design (like a snowflake or a triangle or something), but it didn't draw a fractal.
    Potential Solution: Try changing all the turns to a new angle, or try setting some to one angle and others to a different angle.

How can I speed up/slow down the animation?

If the animation is happening, but going too slow or too fast, move the mouse pointer over the canvas to reveal the control buttons, then click the buttons with musical notes on them. The button with one note will slow the animation down. The button with two notes will speed the animation up. You can hold the buttons down to make more dramatic speed changes.

There are keyboard shortcuts to accomplish the same thing, but they can be a bit annoying. (See the Controls panel.)

The app froze. What do I do?

If you can still click on the textboxes and make changes, it may be that the animation just paused. To check, move your mouse over the canvas to reveal the control buttons, and click the play button.

If you can't even click on the textboxes anymore, it may be that the app can't handle the fractal you wanted to make. Try lowing the number of iterations (in the "Iterations" box). The more iterations you request, the longer the string of instructions that the app has to process becomes (usually). Having to process more instructions takes up more time and memory.

What's the difference between "Lindenmayer" and "Prime Numbers" modes?

Both use a string of symbols (a sequence of letters or numbers), and both treat that string of symbols as a list of instructions for drawing a line. In the Lindenmayer mode, you get to construct that string of symbols yourself. In the Prime Numbers mode, the string of symbols is determined by the prime factorizations of the positive integers. However, even in the Prime Numbers mode, you get to choose which prime numbers to use and what angles to use.

For more background info on the two modes, close this panel, then click the "Info" button.

What's the difference between "Animate Final Iteration" and "Show Iteration Sequence" options?

A Lindenmayer system is, first and foremost, a set of rules for transforming one string of symbols into another. For example, it might turn "x" into "xyx," then turn "xyx" into "xyxyyxyx," then turn "xyxyyxyx" into "xyxyyxyxyyyyxyxyyxyx," and so on. Each of these transformations is an "iteration."

Each of those iterations produces a string of symbols that the app interprets as a series of instructions for drawing a line. The "Animate Final Iteration" vs. "Show Iteration Sequence" menu allows you to tell the app whether to show you only the line the app would draw when it follows the final set of instructions, or whether to show you the sequence of lines it would draw if it drew a new line for each iteration.

For example, if "x" means "step forward, drawing a line," and "y" means "turn right, 90 degrees," then "Show Iteration Sequence" would go like this:

  1. "x"(the app would draw a horizontal line segment)
  2. "xyx"(the app would draw that same line segment, but then turn right 90 degrees [to face "down"] and draw a vertical line segment)
  3. "xyxyyxyx"             (the app would draw the same two line segments it drew last time [based on the "xyx" part of the string], but then would turn around to face up [based on the "yy" part of the string, which is two 90-degree turns], and then draw an upside-down "L" [based on the second "xyx" part of the string]; the result would look like a "T".)
  4. "xyxyyxyxyyyyxyxyyxyx" (the app would draw the same "T" as before [based on the "xyxyyxyx" part], then turn 360 degrees [based on the "yyyy" part], then draw another "T" [based on the second "xyxyyxyx"])

However, if the app is set to "Animate Final Iteration," it will jump right to the "xyxyyxyxyyyyxyxyyxyx" version of the string. It will then follow that string's instruction for drawing a line, over and over again, gradually increasing the angle it turns each time.

  1. "xyxyyxyxyyyyxyxyyxyx";   1st "frame" of animation: Angle = 0.0 degrees (the app would draw a horizontal line segment, turn right 0 degrees, draw another horizontal line segment, turn right 0 degrees, turn right 0 degrees, draw another horizontal line segment, etc. The result will look like a single, horizontal line)
  2. "xyxyyxyxyyyyxyxyyxyx";   2nd "frame" of animation: Angle = 0.1 degrees (the app would draw a horizontal line segment, turn right 0.1 degrees, draw another almost-horizontal line segment, turn right 0.1 degrees, turn right 0.1 degrees, draw another line segment, etc. The result will look like a mostly-horizontal line that is curving ever-so-slightly downward.)
  3. "xyxyyxyxyyyyxyxyyxyx";   3rd "frame" of animation: Angle = 0.2 degrees (the app would draw a horizontal line segment, turn right 0.2 degrees, draw another line segment, turn right 0.2 degrees, turn right 0.2 degrees, draw another line segment, etc. The result will still look mostly horizontal, but will curve slightly downward even more.)
  4. . . .
  5. "xyxyyxyxyyyyxyxyyxyx";899th "frame" of animation: Angle = 89.8 degrees (the app would draw a horizontal line segment, turn right 89.8 degrees, draw another line segment, turn right 89.8 degrees, turn right 89.8 degrees, draw another line segment, etc. The result will look almost like two Ts, but each "T" will look like it has been split down the middle ever so slightly -- as if its vertical line was a "V" that had almost closed)
  6. "xyxyyxyxyyyyxyxyyxyx";900th "frame" of animation: Angle = 89.9 degrees (the app would draw a horizontal line segment, turn right 89.9 degrees, draw another line segment, turn right 89.9 degrees, turn right 89.9 degrees, draw another line segment, etc. The result will look almost like two Ts, with the "split" down the middle of both Ts being almost completely closed.)
  7. "xyxyyxyxyyyyxyxyyxyx";901st "frame" of animation: Angle = 90.0 degrees (the app would draw a horizontal line segment, turn right 90.0 degrees, draw another line segment, turn right 90.0 degrees, turn right 90.0 degrees, draw another line segment, etc. The result will look like two Ts: "TT")
